Antiwork, because ambition is overrated.

From antiwork in the USA to “tang ping” (躺平) or “lying flat” in China, people are fed up with the grind. We shouldn't live to work, but work to live; we shouldn't serve the economy, the economy should serve us.

Working hard ánd being antiwork?

I enjoy working hard, especially toward a common good, a noble goal, or simply helping a friend. The fruits of labour are immediately visible, and don't simply increase the profits of some intangible entity or harmful industry. Unfortunately the latter cases are ubiquitous; advertising, factory farming, car-centrism, financial speculation, exorbitant symptom suppressing instead of curative medicine, et cetera. Pardon my penchant for my black and white rhetoric, obviously not everything is bad. For instance, I have an indomitable esteem for good doctors or surgeons, even though some do it for the wrong reason. The fact that one can shatter a leg while We've evermore become cogs in a profit squeezing machine: unappreciated, exploited, and replaceable. “But reduced pov…” No. Reduced poverty through capitalism is a lie [] to justify our bukkake of luxury.

Perhaps that's why ever since I can remember I've never liked the thought of work, or, “a job”. The same routine five days a week, seeing colleagues more than my friends, for the rest of my life … Predictable and thus boring. Especially considering the unfathomable amount of more interesting things to learn or do. Unless of course one is lucky enough to stumble upon and make ends meet with their dream job. But that's not “a job” anymore—something one is obliged to do—but rather a vocation—something one wants to do. Naturally every profession will have its moments of drudgery, but in general a vocation does not feel like a chore. Unlike being a cog.

This is a remarkably touchy subject matter in several developed countries. “Cultures”* where people often take pride in their work regardless whether they enjoy it or its net negative consequences. In such work-obsessed societies it is often considered sacrilege to be critical of work. Therefore, I'll let one of the greatest minds do some unpaid work for me. I'm sorry Albert, “you're doing it for the exposure” [].

“Einstein struck a more serious pose when he addressed the Caltech student body near the end of his stay. His sermon, grounded in his humanistic outlook, was on how science had not yet been harnessed to do more good than harm.
