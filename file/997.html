Prime 997 revisited.

Divisible only by itself and one. Remarkably simple, but simply amazing.

996 is not prime.

996 = 1 × 996 = 2 × 498 = 3 × 332 = 4 × 249 = 6 × 166 = 12 × 83 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 83. Simply put, it is possible to represent a collection of 996 things as multiple equal collections of a smaller size. For instance, below is 996 represented as 3 collections of 332 letters “i”.

Okay, so what?

997 is prime.

997 = 1 × 997. That's it. Merely adding 1 to 996 makes a collection of 997 things, a collection that is impossible to represent as any other amount of equal collections of a smaller size.

{iiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiiii}.

What the heck?

Infinity and beyond.

Remarkable. But now consider the prime 67 280 421 310 721, which reads “67 trillion 280 billion 421 million 310 thousand 721”, yes, also indivisible. Let's go even bigger. The current largest known (Mersenne) prime is:

282589933 - 1 = 148894445742041325547 … (24 862 006 digits omitted) … 037951210325217902591.

So, primes can be unfathomably huge, are infinite in supply [], yet remain indivisible. Seriously, how?
